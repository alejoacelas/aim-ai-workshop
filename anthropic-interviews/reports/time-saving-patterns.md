# How non-programmers save time with AI

An analysis of 1,220 interview transcripts from the AnthropicInterviewer dataset, focused on professionals outside software engineering.

---

## 1. Executive summary

Across more than a thousand interviews, a clear pattern emerges: non-programmers are saving the most time on documentation, communication, and research—not on the "core" work they were hired to do. Teachers, lawyers, writers, therapists, healthcare workers, retail employees, and freelancers describe AI as a tool that compresses administrative overhead, often dramatically. The most commonly cited savings cluster around email drafting, report writing, lesson planning, and initial research, with specific claims ranging from 10x speedups on clinical notes to week-long legal proposals completed in a single day.

The savings are real but uneven. Professionals who bring strong domain expertise and treat AI as a first-draft generator or research accelerator report the highest satisfaction and the largest time reductions. Those who attempt to use AI for tasks requiring personal voice, creative judgment, or high factual accuracy frequently describe a net time loss after accounting for verification, editing, and correction. The "verification tax"—time spent checking AI output—is nearly universal, with several respondents citing error rates of 50% or higher on specific tasks like spreadsheet formulas and legal citations.

Perhaps most notably, the interviews reveal that time savings often do not simply translate to "doing the same work faster." Instead, many professionals describe a qualitative shift: teachers produce more observations per student, lawyers rethink their billing models, and freelance writers take on a higher volume of assignments. The freed-up time is being reinvested, not banked. But this reinvestment carries its own risks—several respondents worry about deskilling, loss of learning, and the creeping normalization of AI-generated mediocrity.

## 2. Taxonomy of time-saving patterns

### 2.1 First-draft generation

The single most common time-saving pattern. Professionals provide bullet points, rough notes, or key parameters and have AI produce a structured first draft that they then edit. This applies to emails, reports, lesson plans, proposals, and clinical documentation.

> "I use AI basically daily. I am an Early Years Educator, and I take observations daily of the children's learning. These observations must be written with the activity the child has undertaken, what skills they have learned from it, and any next steps for learning or any ideas for extending the learning. There's a pretty specific format. I have fed an AI examples of my own personal observations as an example template, and the AI now uses this template to write my observations for me with me giving it my bullet point notes on what happened. It saves me SO MUCH TIME. Before it took me 30 minutes to complete one, now it's not even 5."
> — work_0245

> "I recently had to prepare a detailed proposal for a client that was considering retaining our firm in a complicated regulatory matter.  It is the kind of proposal that, in the past, would have taken me several days to a week to prepare given the complexity of the matter an the client's specfiic requirements.  However, because I had some extensive rough notes, I was able to upload my notes directly from [REDACTED] into Copilot's Researcher agent and generate a proposal to the client that was around 80% complete in 10 minutes."
> — work_0450

### 2.2 Communication polishing

Professionals use AI to take rough, emotionally charged, or poorly structured messages and transform them into professional communications. This is especially valued by people who self-identify as struggling with written communication or who work under time pressure.

> "The use of AI to quickly draft update emails to clients about quite minor details has been most satisfying - when I take about 5-10 minutes to draft an email, and there is 7-8 of them a day, the time savings are significant."
> — work_0577

> "I have a tendency to avoid being direct with people when I'm afraid of hurting their feelings or sounding too harsh. AI is really good at helping me strike a balance between being upfront and clear, without being avoidant or harsh."
> — work_0657

### 2.3 Research acceleration

AI compresses the initial research phase—finding relevant sources, identifying products, locating obscure information—even when every result must be verified manually.

> "Well it would have involved reading 10k/10q reports and other company filings, looking at historical financial data, reading the news etc to learn about companies, now I get a list that is worth looking into further, with much of the data and info I need presented for them already via the agent report. I guess the report from the agent saves me probably 3-7 hours a week."
> — work_0838

> "When it comes to doing product research I used to spend hours even days trying to see what products were selling and what other sellers where doing better. This was cut short by more than half the time using everbee"
> — work_0904

### 2.4 Template and structure creation

Rather than generating final content, AI creates reusable frameworks: spreadsheet templates, lesson plan structures, document outlines, email templates for bulk communication.

> "Saving time generating templates for bulk email dissemination is very satisfying, saving a significant amount of time."
> — work_0886

> "I feel particularly satisfied really anytime that I'm creating Excel spreadsheets. The data is usually transferred really well so that it doesn't take much formatting if any to get to the final product."
> — work_0352

### 2.5 Knowledge-on-demand

AI serves as an instant, contextualized reference—a replacement for searching documentation, watching tutorials, or asking colleagues. Particularly valued by people working alone or in understaffed environments.

> "The only other way of learning this is through courses or Youtube videos, which is time intensive and you're dealing with ads or a cost and you cant ask questions - with AI you get an answer immediately, specifically tailored to your data or task."
> — work_0178

> "It felt like when they download skills in The Matrix."
> — work_0745

### 2.6 Administrative automation by non-coders

Non-programmers use AI to build small tools, scripts, or automations they could never have created themselves, eliminating recurring manual tasks entirely.

> "I recently used AI to write a little app that makes massive formatting changes to different documents and then leverages an open source python module to convert the doc to a different file type. I was able to get the app up and running in about an hour. The app takes about 1 minute to run. I think it probably would have taken me 6+ hours to make the changes to these docs myself."
> — work_0108

### 2.7 Real-time transcription and note-taking

AI listens, transcribes, and reformats speech into structured professional documentation, especially in clinical and therapeutic settings.

> "We wear microphones and describe each step of what we are doing, and the AI helps measure time duration and repetitions. It then writes them in our notes in an easy way to read and review"
> — work_0478

## 4. Most surprising and counterintuitive patterns

### AI as emotional buffer

Several respondents described using AI not for speed but for emotional regulation. A customer service agent used it to defuse their own frustration during difficult calls; a manager used it to craft sensitive messages they were otherwise avoiding.

> "I needed analogies for them. I asked for analogies and asked ChatGPT for an explanation suitable to an 8 year old (the cx was in their 40s). I needed to detach my own ego from the situation (why wasn't MY words working) and just offer something different and the use of this tool helped me remain calm in the moment."
> — work_0069

### AI as replacement for missing colleagues

Remote workers and solo entrepreneurs explicitly describe AI as a substitute for the in-person collaboration they lost. One small-business owner recreated the collaborative problem-solving environment of a previous office job.

> "I work remotely quite a bit (post-COVID), connecting with my team throughout the day, but have really missed the in-office opportunities for collaboration. AI has provided another way for me to collaborate when missing the in-person brainstorming."
> — work_0657

> "Before creating my own business I used to work at an office with many employees... I worked there for four years and was left with that habit. Now that I am on my own I use AI to imitate the way I would solve things in the office."
> — work_0904

### AI supercharges ADHD

Multiple interviewees with ADHD described AI as uniquely well-suited to their cognitive patterns—helping them focus, organize scattered thoughts, and sustain momentum.

> "I think I'm lucky in that I have a theory that AI will usher in an age for people with brains like mine. ADHD, broadly skilled under achievers, who have potential but can't quite apply it. I think AI is most effectively used by holistic thinkers. It super charges ADHD hyperfocus because you suddenly have an expert to talk to about any new thing you pick up."
> — work_0745

### The covert user

Several respondents reported using AI secretly at work—either because it is officially discouraged, or because they feared social judgment from colleagues.

> "I now keep my use of the tools quiet as I'm sure it would be frowned upon. But the tools I do have are not up to scratch, so I am just trying to meet the pressures of the job with some problem solving, its not my fault their systems are so outdated, I shouldn't suffer and neither should cxs"
> — work_0069

> "I've also had praise on the quality of my written work, I suppose I'd feel a little silly if they realised that it was actually an AI that wrote it for me!"
> — work_0245

### The billable hour crisis

A lawyer described how AI-driven efficiency is forcing a rethinking of the billable-hour model—a fundamental disruption to how legal services are priced.

> "The standard billing method for decades for Canadian law firms has been the billable hour.  The rise of AI is threatening the billable hour like never before since a task that used to take 10 hours, for example, may now be complete in 2 hours, with a corresponding reduction in billable hours.  Therefore, we are having discussions in the firm about whether it makes sense to bill some items on a fixed fee basis that reflects the value we provide to the client."
> — work_0450

## 5. Workflow transformations (before vs. after)

### Teacher writing student reports

**Before:** A primary school team leader would spend 10–20 hours writing individualized semester reports for 30 children, composing each one from scratch based on curriculum standards and student progress.

**After:** The teacher inputs parameters (200-word limit, 2 curriculum achievement standards, a next step, a home practice idea, age-appropriate language) and AI generates a draft that the teacher reviews and personalizes.

> "And when you have 30 children to write about, this is incredibly time saving."
> — work_0038

### Canine rehabilitation therapist documenting sessions

**Before:** After a 40-minute therapy session with a dog, the therapist would spend another 40 minutes writing clinical notes from memory, struggling to recall exact durations and repetition counts.

**After:** The therapist wears a microphone during the session, narrating naturally ("come on fido, let's climb up on this ball! Good, hold it there for a few moments"), and the AI transcribes it into clinical language ("Fido was able to climb on the ball and remain there for 7 seconds. Exercise was repeated 3 times"). Notes now take 5–10 minutes.

> "The more time I have to help clients and not do the paperwork, the better for everyone"
> — work_0478

### Customer service agent drafting follow-up emails

**Before:** A telecom customer service agent used a broken internal "wizard" system to compose follow-up emails, describing the process as so painful they would "do all I could to avoid an email."

**After:** AI generates the email body from a few key details; the agent reviews, tweaks, and sends. The process went from dread-inducing to routine.

> "The emails, when I realised how much time I was saving, vs what I had been doing before. I can skip a lengthy (and I really do mean lengthy) process and go straight to the email address and copy paste in the Output."
> — work_0069

### Freelance photographer editing garment images

**Before:** Editing a product photo to add details like bias tape and embroidered hearts required hours of searching for reference images, clipping, color matching, and blending to avoid a "pasted on" look.

**After:** Photoshop AI generates the edits directly in context. After prompt refinement, the same edit takes 15 minutes.

> "If I had to do this 'by hand' it would have taken me a couple of hours, searching for images of the things that needed to be added and making them fit into the existing image without looking like they were pasted on. I used Photoshop AI to generate the edits to the garment, and after some fiddling with my prompts I was finished with the edits in 15 minutes instead of hours."
> — creativity_0077

## 6. Which tasks see the biggest time ROI?

Ranked by consistency of reported savings across interviews:

1. **Structured documentation from notes/bullets** (clinical notes, student observations, progress reports). Savings: 4–8x. The highest and most consistent ROI because the task is formulaic, the input is well-defined, and the output format is predictable.

2. **Professional email drafting** (especially repetitive communications). Savings: 2–5x. Nearly every non-programmer who mentioned email drafting reported satisfaction. The task is low-stakes, short-form, and benefits enormously from AI's ability to shift tone.

3. **Initial research and information gathering** (finding products, companies, case law, academic sources). Savings: 2–5x. High ROI on speed, but partially offset by the verification tax.

4. **Lesson and curriculum planning** (for teachers). Savings: 2–5x. Teachers are among the most enthusiastic adopters, particularly for generating activity ideas and structuring lesson plans.

5. **Spreadsheet formulas and data manipulation**. Savings: variable (sometimes days reduced to hours). High ROI when it works, but reported reliability is low—one respondent cited a 50% success rate.

6. **Creative brief execution** (turning vague instructions into structured outlines, slide decks, document frameworks). Savings: 1.5–3x. Useful as a starting point, but almost always requires significant revision.

7. **Code/automation creation by non-coders**. Savings: potentially infinite (from "impossible" to "done in an hour"), but requires troubleshooting tolerance and is inconsistent.

## 7. Hidden costs and time sinks

### The verification tax

Nearly every interviewee described spending substantial time verifying AI output. For some, this partially or entirely erases the time savings.

> "It's now more of norm in daily use so I don't get the 'satisfied' feeling I used to when I learned how much time I could save. But frustrations are a daily occurrence. It still has a long way to go, it can't analyse large datasets on excel, it often can't complete what seem to be the simplest tasks. I don't quite trust it, I have to always ask for sources, and open another chat to make the same request for data to check its answers. Still, I find maybe 60% of the time there is an issue - 100% if we're reflecting back over all of the conversations each day."
> — work_0178

> "Yes, sometimes it doesn't correctly call back to certain cells, rows, columns so I always have do double check. I'd say 50% of the time its right & there's no need for me to go back & forth to correct the mistakes."
> — work_0416

### Fabricated citations

Hallucinated references are a persistent problem, especially for teachers, writers, and researchers. Time spent tracking down non-existent sources is pure waste.

> "I can share a time where it made me frustrated. I was writing a research paper and I needed some citations, so I asked the model to give me some research journals I could look up and read. It gave me a list, but then I couldnt find like 90% of them because they were fabricated. So it bothered me that the model made up the references instead of guiding me to real research that exists."
> — work_0778

### The "doubling the workload" problem

When AI output requires extensive rewriting to match a personal voice, the total time can exceed doing the work from scratch.

> "I started with a base of knowledge, indicated to the AI to summarise it and how - but then still needed to spend time making sure it was correct, and adapting it to my voice...and I felt like I had wasted time and could have just done it from the get go."
> — work_0081

### Context window degradation

Long conversations with AI produce diminishing returns as the model loses track of earlier instructions.

> "I also learned to start new conversations every so often to avoid running up too many interactions in the one chat? Sometimes if you talk in one chat for too long the quality seems to disintegrate."
> — work_0392

### The learning cost tradeoff

Using AI for summarization or research means skipping the cognitive work that builds expertise. Several respondents flagged this tension explicitly.

> "When you summarise yourself, the information tends to stick more. It's like when you are studying for an exam and revise information and knowledge, and practice. That is completely lost if an AI is doing the revision or summarisation for you - as you are repeating the information from the sources, and skipping a step."
> — work_0081

### Template/prompt development overhead

Setting up AI to work correctly for a specific workflow requires significant upfront investment that is rarely discussed.

> "When I am developing a new template to use for the structure of a session, it is frustrating to describe to the AI the type of information I want them to listen for and how to transcribe it into the notes. I wish there was an easier way to make these templates so future notes are easier. Right now it is a trial and error approach that can take a lot of time, until the template is achieved. Then it is fast"
> — work_0478

### Colleague resistance creates a hidden social cost

Using AI can create friction with colleagues who view it as "cheating."

> "My colleagues are not as positive about AI as I am. They think it's cheating not to write your own work and they won't even use it for simple blogs."
> — work_0392

> "A lot of people in this field believe that if you need an AI to do the work for you, then you shouldn't be doing it in the first place."
> — work_0245

## 8. Key quotes collection

### On the nature of collaboration

> "I use AI mainly as a collaboration partner. I will ask it to tell me what I am missing, explain why something may or may not be a good idea, flesh out something I have already established a framework for, or grade my work/progress. I rarely assign it tasks due to trust issues."
> — work_0173

> "I use it more about being given more diverse ideas I may not have thought about and less about how it should be delivered and big thinking questions and ideas."
> — work_0038

### On what AI cannot replace

> "At the end of the day teaching is a human endeavour and the personable element needs to be done by a human."
> — work_0038

> "The first thing I can think of is writing emails. I can see the appeal in certain contexts where its really not worth the time and effort... but I'm not a massive fan of the idea that its just bots talking to bots all the time"
> — work_0882

> "Writing. AI can write but it isn't quite there yet. Reading AI content lacks the nuance of human written words. I can pick it out AI generated text very easily, and automatically I feel that it has less value - its a shortcut. It lacks that intentionality you mentioned above - like a factory pumping out cheap goods - no one wants this."
> — work_0178

### On trust and verification

> "I am not so much worried about 'maintaining my skills' as a lawyer.  However, the time savings are having an impact on the economics of running a law firm, particularly a small firm."
> — work_0450

> "I don't think AI can fully take over my profession and others like it; I think it eliminates the low performers and empowers the high performers."
> — work_0082

### On the experience of time savings

> "Overall, the time to write an article I would say is 25 to 50% faster, but actually, the main benefit is cognitive flow. If I'm stuck on a part (outlining, researching, writing a section) I can always just ask the AI to give it a try. Sometimes the result is beneficial, sometimes not, but it helps keep things moving if I'm 'dreading the blank page,' so to speak."
> — creativity_0044

> "I felt super satisfied recently when I was doing some thematic analysis with a group of interviews. There was so much content and I was getting really overwhelmed. This is usually when I would sit down with a teammate and start mind mapping together. I fed some of the quotes I had collected from the interviews and asked the AI to help me develop some themes and subthemes and start grouping the quotes. Once we had the skeleton, I used the AI as a sounding board to refine and work through the material until I had a pretty substantial framework. It really cut my worktime in half and made me feel less alone in the process."
> — work_0657

### On fear and the future

> "I think it's a great resource but my worry is it will cause teaching to merely be about content delivery and we will take the humanity, artistry and creativity out of teaching. We already have a generation of ipad kids and do we really want that to be our future of learning? I'm not keen to see a Wall-E-esque future of learning for our children."
> — work_0038

> "AI is becoming a large part of the teacher toolkit due to its ability to take time consuming thought processes and break them down into simple ideas. Most teachers are beginning to come on boars due to work load pressures and finding different ways to enhance their knowledge of topics before teaching them."
> — work_0038

> "I know it coming for my job, and I'm not overly bothered. The telecomms industry cycles through needing UK agents and then offshoring the work when their profits suffer too much. But then profits start suffering because cxs want UK based agents. AI agents will just be another step in this cycle."
> — work_0069

> "I will always value human skills more - I think that even though AI can pump out tasks - we still pay more for handcrafted items, they are still viewed with more value than a machine made item - so holding onto my skills is more about maintaining that value and developing."
> — work_0178
